Chapter 7 — Photosynthesis: How Plants Make Their Own Food

Plants are unique among living things because they can make their own food. This process is called photosynthesis. The word means "putting together with light."

Where photosynthesis happens
Photosynthesis takes place inside small green structures called chloroplasts. Chloroplasts contain a green pigment called chlorophyll. Chlorophyll captures energy from sunlight. Most chloroplasts are found in the leaves of plants, which is why leaves are usually green.

The ingredients
To carry out photosynthesis, plants need three things:
1. Sunlight (energy)
2. Water (absorbed by the roots)
3. Carbon dioxide (taken in from the air through tiny pores called stomata)

The products
Photosynthesis turns these ingredients into two things:
1. Glucose, a sugar that the plant uses as food
2. Oxygen, which the plant releases back into the air

The light reaction and the Calvin cycle
Scientists divide photosynthesis into two stages. The light reaction uses sunlight to split water molecules and produce energy carriers. The Calvin cycle uses that energy to combine carbon dioxide with hydrogen, building glucose one carbon at a time. The Calvin cycle does not need light directly, but it depends on the energy made in the light reaction.

Why photosynthesis matters
Almost every living thing depends on photosynthesis. Animals eat plants for energy, and the oxygen we breathe was originally released by photosynthetic organisms. Without photosynthesis, Earth's atmosphere would not have enough oxygen to support animal life.

Common misconceptions
- Plants do not "eat" the soil. They take in water and minerals from soil, but their food (glucose) is made inside the leaves.
- Trees do not produce their oxygen at night. The light reaction only runs in sunlight; at night, plants release a small amount of carbon dioxide as they respire.
- Chlorophyll is not the only pigment in plants. Leaves contain other pigments, which is why some turn red, orange, or yellow in autumn.
